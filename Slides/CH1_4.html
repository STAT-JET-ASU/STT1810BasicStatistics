Section 1.4 New Vocabulary

two-sided test / alternative hypothesis: a test of significance in which the alternative hypothesis Ha has the form “not equal to” or “does not equal” (≠) rather than less or greater (< or >).

Example: Our online ESP test mentions both “psi-hitting” (getting more cards correct than expected by chance) and “psi-missing” (getting fewer correct). If we are interested in either of the two possibilities, we would write our hypotheses as follows.

\[H_0: \pi = 0.20\]

\[H_a: \pi \neq 0.20\]

In this case, “extreme” would be measured on both sides of the null distribution, not just the upper side like \(H_a: \pi > 0.20\).

What Impacts Strength of Evidence?

Three important components of a test of significance influence the strength provided by the data evidence.

  • extremeness: The farther away the observed statistic is from the average value of the null distribution (i.e., the value that is specified for \(\pi\) in Ho), the more evidence there is against \(H_0\).

  • sample size: As the sample size increases (while the values of the observed sample statistic \(\hat{p}\) and \(H_a\) stay the same) the strength of evidence against \(H_0\) increases.

  • one- vs. two-tailed Ha: Because the p-value for a two-sided test is about twice as large as we would get for a one-sided test using the same data, it provides less evidence against \(H_0\). (Two-sided tests are used more often in scientific practice.)

Flashback: ESP Test

Example: We can use the One Proportion Applet to assess the statistical significance of our Zener card ESP test results from earlier in the semester.

\[H_o: \pi = 0.20\]

\[H_a: \pi > 0.20\]

Let’s say my results were 6 out of 25 cards correct.

  • What is the p-value for this particular test result?
  • What if I’d scored 9 correct instead of 6 on the test?
  • What if I’d done a test with 50 cards and got 12 right?
  • What if I’d tested for both psi-hitting and psi-missing?

INITIAL: I got 6 of 25 cards correct on my ESP test, so \(\hat{p} = 0.24\).

⬤ I got 9 of 25 cards correct, so \(n\) is still \(25\), but now \(\hat{p} = 0.36\).

⬤ I got 12 of 50 cards correct, so \(\hat{p}\) is still \(0.24\), but now \(n = 50\).

⬤ I used a two-sided hypothesis instead of one on the initial test.

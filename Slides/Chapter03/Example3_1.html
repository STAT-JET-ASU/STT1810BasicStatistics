Overview of the Study

Researchers tested a dog named Marine to see whether or not she could detect colorectal cancer via smell. She sniffed breath in 5 bags from 5 different patients, only 1 of which had cancer. This process was repeated many times. Marine chose correctly in 30 out of 33 trials (sets of bags), so \(\hat{p} = 0.909\).

\(H_0: \pi = 0.20\) (Marine is just guessing, 1 in 5 chance)
\(H_a: \pi > 0.20\) (Marine is doing better than guessing)

We would reject \(H_0\) at the 0.05 level of significance. But, what is Marines true probability \(\pi\) of detecting cancer? We can estimate the value by testing many potential values of \(\pi\) on either side of our observed sample proportion \(\hat{p} = 0.909\).

Two-sided p-value 0.044 0.068 0.071 0.059 0.058 0.043

We are 95% confident that Marines true probability of detecting cancer (\(\pi\)) is between \(0.761\) and \(0.974\). Much better than \(20\%\)! This is more informative than simply concluding \(\pi > 0.20\) (\(H_a\)).

The Test for \(H_0: \pi = 0.760\)

The two-sided p-value is less than 0.05, so we reject \(0.760\) as a plausible value for \(\pi\). It will not be in the confidence interval.

The Test for \(H_0: \pi = 0.761\)

The two-sided p-value is greater than 0.05, so \(\pi = 0.761\) will be considered a plausible value. It will be in the interval.

The Test for \(H_0: \pi = 0.909\)

When \(\pi = \hat{p}\), the p-value is 1. Note that all tests use \(\hat{p} = 0.909\) to assess the hypothesized value of \(\pi\). The data is our evidence.

The Test for \(H_0: \pi = 0.974\)

The two-sided p-value is greater than 0.05, so \(\pi = 0.974\) will be considered a plausible value. It will be in the interval.

The Test for \(H_0: \pi = 0.975\)

The two-sided p-value is less than 0.05, so we reject \(0.975\) as a plausible value for \(\pi\). It will not be in the confidence interval.

How Many Tests?

In this context, finding a 95% confidence interval with 3 decimal places, using the method of plausible values, would require over 200 significance tests like the ones on the previous slidesone for every potential value of \(\pi\) we need to evaluate to determine the endpoints of the interval. Using logic, we can reduce this.

A 99% confidence interval determined by this method would be wider and require even more tests! More confidence means the interval will contain more plausible values.

So why did we do this? The point is to connect our significance testing to confidence intervals, and level of significance to level of confidence. The two methods are inherently connected.

Since we used the standard p-value \(\leq 0.05\) rule to reject \(H_0\) in all of our tests, the level of confidence is \(100\% - 5\% = 95\%\).
